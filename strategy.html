01 — Executive Summary

The opportunity in one page

GOAL already produces genuinely excellent client resources. The problem is that none of them live on checkoutgoal.com — so they build authority, traffic, and trust for everyone except GOAL. The redesign is the moment to fix that.

Today, checkoutgoal.com is a classic brochure site: a handful of static HTML pages whose only job is to book a demo. It does that job, but it captures none of the demand from agency owners who are searching for help before they're ready to buy — and it gives existing clients nowhere to learn. Meanwhile, two best-in-class guides already exist (a 30-day lead follow-up playbook and a CRM data-export hub), but they're stranded on disconnected vercel.app URLs that earn GOAL nothing in search and look like side projects rather than part of the platform.

The core recommendation

Launch a first-class Resources Hub at checkoutgoal.com/resources/ as part of the redesign. Migrate the two existing guides onto the domain, wrap them in a consistent IA and content system, and expand into a library that maps to how insurance agencies actually buy and operate. Treat copy as the product: every title, hub card, intro, and schema block is written to be found, trusted, and acted on.

This brief is deliberately weighted toward content and copy — the redesign team will own visual design and build, so the highest-leverage thing this document can provide is the words: the hub copy, the per-guide title/meta/H1 formulas, the card patterns, the schema, the internal-linking plan, and a prioritized content roadmap grounded in GOAL's real product surface area.

The three jobs the Resources section does

  • Acquisition — capture agency owners searching operational questions GOAL is uniquely qualified to answer ("how to follow up on internet insurance leads," "best crm for insurance agency leads") and route them toward a demo.
  • Sales enablement — give account managers and the demo flow credible, linkable proof that GOAL is an operating partner, not just a lead vendor.
  • Retention & success — clients who follow the follow-up cadence and export their data cleanly convert more, attribute results correctly, and stay. Resources are a churn lever, not just a marketing asset.

03 — The Strategic Case

Why a Resources section, and why now

A modern SaaS site earns trust before it asks for a demo. For GOAL specifically — selling to skeptical, time-poor insurance agency owners — demonstrated operating expertise is the differentiator that aggregators can't copy.

The "stranded authority" problem

GOAL's best content currently builds equity for a throwaway domain instead of the brand. The redesign is the one-time opportunity to bring it home.

Today

Authority leaks off-domain

checkoutgoal.com — brochure only
— no link —
goal-followup-best-practices.vercel.app
lead-export-guides.vercel.app

Guides rank (if at all) for a domain GOAL doesn't market. No internal links, no shared analytics, no brand reinforcement, no path to a demo.

After redesign

Authority compounds on-brand

checkoutgoal.com/resources/
↳ links up & down
/resources/lead-follow-up-playbook
/resources/guides/export-data

Every guide strengthens the domain that ranks for "insurance leads," cross-links to money pages, and carries a consistent demo CTA.

Why content fits GOAL's buyer in particular

  • The buyer is mid-task, not mid-funnel. Agency owners search for fixes to real problems — "my internet leads don't answer," "how do I export bind data to AgencyZoom." GOAL's product is the answer to those problems; the content is just the honest demonstration of it.
  • Trust is the whole sale. The market is burned by shared-lead aggregators. A library that openly teaches follow-up discipline and clean data hygiene signals "partner," not "vendor" — and directly supports the existing "old way vs. the GOAL way" narrative.
  • AI assistants are the new top of funnel. Owners increasingly ask ChatGPT, Perplexity, and Google's AI overviews "how should I follow up on insurance leads?" Well-structured, schema-marked guides are how GOAL becomes the cited source in those answers (see §05, AI visibility).
  • Resources reduce churn. The follow-up playbook isn't marketing — it's the difference between a client blaming "bad leads" and a client hitting 3–5× median bind rates. Better-performing clients renew. Content is a retention investment with a marketing dividend.

GOAL doesn't need to invent a content strategy. It needs to stop hiding the one it already has.

What "good" looks like

The proof points already exist in GOAL's own work: the follow-up playbook's data-backed, script-included depth and the export hub's scannable, CRM-by-CRM card grid with a "Don't see your CRM?" expansion hook. The strategy is to systematize that quality across a full library — same voice, same usefulness, now on-domain and discoverable.

04 — Information Architecture

How the Resources section is structured

A shallow, scannable hub-and-spoke. One Resources home, a small set of durable categories, and individual guides underneath. URLs are clean, keyword-bearing, and stable so they accrue authority over time.

Recommended URL & page structure

checkoutgoal.com/resources/ # Resources Hub — the landing/index ├─ /resources/playbooks/ # category: strategy & best practices │ ├─ /resources/playbooks/30-day-lead-follow-up # ← migrate existing guide │ ├─ /resources/playbooks/speed-to-lead │ └─ /resources/playbooks/aged-lead-revival ├─ /resources/guides/ # category: CRM & data how-tos │ ├─ /resources/guides/export-lead-data # ← migrate existing hub │ ├─ /resources/guides/export/agencyzoom │ ├─ /resources/guides/export/ricochet │ └─ /resources/guides/export/lead-manager ├─ /resources/verticals/ # category: by line of business │ ├─ /resources/verticals/auto-insurance-leads │ ├─ /resources/verticals/home-insurance-leads │ └─ /resources/verticals/medicare-leads ├─ /resources/product/ # category: using GOAL (help/docs) │ ├─ /resources/product/quotefunnel-setup │ └─ /resources/product/reading-your-reports └─ /resources/glossary/ # definitions — long-tail + AI citations └─ /resources/glossary/[term]
IA principles

1. Keep it two clicks deep. Hub → guide. Categories are filters/sections on the hub, not mandatory click-throughs. 2. URLs describe content, not format — /lead-follow-up not /blog/post-1. 3. Never change a published URL. If a guide moves, 301-redirect. 4. Every guide links up to its category/hub and across to 2–3 sibling guides + one money page.

Hub page anatomy (what's on /resources/)

  1. Hero — headline + one-line promise + (optional) search.
  2. Featured / start-here — the follow-up playbook, given pride of place.
  3. Category sections — Playbooks · Guides · By Line of Business · Using GOAL, each a card grid (reuse the proven export-hub card pattern).
  4. "Don't see what you need?" request hook (proven engagement device from the export hub).
  5. Demo CTA band — consistent conversion path at the bottom of every resource page.

Note: the GOAL Guides Hub design reference (Claude Design) is intended to be the visual spec for this hub page. Reading and implementing it is gated on connector authorization — see the Appendix for the exact handoff.

5.5 — Internal linking plan

Internal links are how authority flows from content to the pages that convert. Every resource page carries the same three link obligations:

Link directionFrom → ToAnchor-text guidance
UpGuide → its category → /resources/ (breadcrumb)Exact category name. "Playbooks", "CRM & Data Guides".
AcrossGuide → 2–3 sibling guidesDescriptive, varied — "speed-to-lead matters most in the first 5 minutes", not "click here".
Down to money pageGuide → demo / product / pricingContextual + benefit-led — "GOAL delivers exclusive leads you can actually reach". One primary, not five.
From money pagesHomepage / how-it-works → /resources/Add a "Resources" nav item + a "Learn" section. Signals depth to both users and crawlers.
Rule of thumb

Aim for 3–6 contextual internal links per guide, with at least one pointing at a conversion page. Keep total links per page well under 100. Diversify anchor text — repeating the same exact-match anchor everywhere reads as manipulation.

5.6 — AI / answer-engine visibility (GEO)

A growing share of these questions are now answered by ChatGPT, Perplexity, Gemini, and Google AI overviews — not ten blue links. To be the cited source in those answers, write for extraction:

  • Answer in the first 1–2 sentences. Lead each section with the direct answer, then elaborate. AI models lift self-contained, near-the-top statements.
  • Use real numbers and name them. "Leads contacted within 5 minutes are 21× more likely to qualify" is quotable; "respond quickly" is not. GOAL's guides are already rich with these — keep them.
  • Structure for parsing. Clear H2/H3 questions, short paragraphs, ordered steps, and FAQ blocks. The follow-up playbook's numbered 01–11 structure is already ideal.
  • Mark it up. HowTo, FAQPage, and Article schema (§5.4) make content machine-readable.
  • Build entity authority. Consistent Organization schema + a glossary defining GOAL's terms helps engines understand who GOAL is and cite it by name.

5.7 — Voice & copy standards

The two existing guides already establish the voice. Codify it so every new guide sounds like GOAL:

DoDon't
Lead with the agency's problem, in their words.Open with GOAL's product or company history.
Be directive and specific — "call 21+ times in week one."Hedge — "you may want to consider following up."
Back claims with numbers and name the source.Make vibes-based promises ("convert way more!").
Give something usable for free (scripts, checklists, CSV).Gate the useful part behind a demo form.
Earn the CTA at the end, once you've delivered value.Interrupt the guide with sales pitches.
Plain English; define jargon (link the glossary).Acronym soup (RPC, bind rate) with no definition.

07 — Implementation Plan & KPIs

Phasing the build

Four phases, each shippable on its own. The technical SEO checklist (§08) is applied to the new templates in Phase 0 so every page is born optimized — no retrofitting.

Phase 0 · Foundations (with the redesign build)

Make the domain content-ready

  • Add /resources/ to the new site's IA and primary nav.
  • Ship the technical baseline the site lacks today: robots.txt, an XML sitemap (auto-generated), canonical tags, and OG/Twitter defaults — see §08.
  • Build the reusable templates: Hub, Guide, and Card, each wired with the title/meta/H1, schema, and breadcrumb patterns from §05.
  • Implement the GOAL Guides Hub design reference as the hub template (pending connector auth — see Appendix).
Phase 1 · Migrate & launch (weeks 1–2 post-foundation)

Bring the existing guides home + 2 quick wins

  • Move the follow-up playbook and the export hub onto checkoutgoal.com/resources/; 301-redirect the old vercel.app URLs.
  • Rewrite their titles/metas to the §05 templates; add HowTo/Article/FAQ schema.
  • Publish the two P1 net-new playbooks (Speed-to-Lead, Exclusive vs. Shared).
  • Cross-link all four; add the demo CTA band; submit the sitemap.
Phase 2 · Expand (weeks 3–8)

Broaden reach with verticals & product guides

  • Publish the P2 set (Auto / Home / Medicare verticals; QuoteFunnel + Reports guides).
  • Stand up the request hook and start logging guide requests as the demand backlog.
  • Link verticals to the relevant homepage offerings and to the follow-up playbook.
Phase 3 · Scale & compound (ongoing)

Depth, long-tail, and a publishing rhythm

  • Build the glossary; publish demand-led CRM guides and the compliance guide (counsel-reviewed).
  • Set a cadence (e.g., 2 guides/month) and a quarterly refresh of stats and screenshots.
  • Promote top guides through account managers and onboarding as retention assets.
